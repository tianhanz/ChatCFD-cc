This OpenFOAM case simulates the fluidization dynamics within a gas-solid fluidized bed using the `twoPhaseEulerFoam` solver. The simulation involves two distinct phases: an "air" phase representing the gas and a "particles" phase representing the solid granular material. The thermodynamic and transport properties for the gas phase are constant, assuming a perfect gas equation of state, while the solid phase has a constant density and zero viscosity. Gravity acts downward with standard acceleration. 

The interaction between phases is governed by the GidaspowErgunWenYu drag model to resolve particle-fluid forces and the RanzMarshall heat transfer model for thermal coupling. For turbulence modeling, the air phase employs the k-epsilon RANS approach, whereas the particles phase utilizes a kinetic theory model incorporating granular viscosity, conductivity, the Lun granular pressure model, and the JohnsonJackson frictional stress model with specified coefficients including particle-particle restitution.

Discretization employs Euler time integration. Spatial gradients use linear Gauss schemes. Divergence terms are resolved via the van Leer scheme for volume fraction (alpha), limitedLinear schemes for velocities and scalars, and linear Gauss schemes for stress terms. Laplacian and gradient operations use uncorrected or linear Gauss schemes with limited interpolation. 

For solution stability, the PIMPLE algorithm incorporates 3 outer correctors per timestep. Linear solvers utilize: GAMG with DIC smoother for pressure; smoothSolver with symGaussSeidel smoother for velocities, enthalpy, particle granular temperature (Theta), k, and epsilon fields; and specialized treatments for phase fraction fields with implicit pressure coupling and sub-cycling. Most solvers apply tight tolerances (e.g., 1e-8 for pressure) without relaxation. 

Initial conditions define uniform velocity for air and zero velocity for particles, with ambient pressure, specific temperatures per phase, and zero initial phase fractions except solids absent at start. Boundary conditions enforce specialized inlet velocities with interstitial or pressure-driven flow, fixed values for turbulence quantities, calculated pressure, and zero-gradient phase fractions at walls. This setup aims to replicate multiphase flow and heat transfer phenomena characteristic of fluidized beds.
